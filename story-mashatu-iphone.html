The Year I Left
the Camera Behind
Mashatu · Northern Tuli · 2023

Scroll

Mashatu Tent Camp · Main Camp · Tuli Safari Lodge · Northern Tuli Block · 2023

I made a decision before the 2023 trip. A conscious, considered, deliberate decision that I had been thinking about for a long time. I was going to leave the Sony at home. No 200–600mm. No heavy bag. No compulsion to be behind a lens every time something extraordinary happened in front of me. I was going to be present.

It was, as it turned out, one of the most spectacular own goals of my photographic career.

The trip had not started simply. We arrived at Mashatu Tent Camp to find that the temperatures had become extreme — the kind of heat that the Northern Tuli Block occasionally produces in certain seasons. Mashatu moved us. We spent part of the stay at Mashatu Main Camp, the older and more substantial lodge on the same reserve, where the solid walls and deeper shade offered some relief. After Main Camp we continued to our original booking at Tuli Safari Lodge, where we finished the trip.

Dan and his tracker Morris have been reading this landscape together for years. On the drive that changed the trip, they found us two leopards in a tree with an entire herd of elephants moving slowly beneath them. Engine off. Nobody speaking. Dan had positioned us exactly where we needed to be.

I had my iPhone.

Here is what I have learned about iPhone photography in the African bush: it forces you to be honest. You cannot reach across fifty metres with a long lens and isolate a face in shallow depth of field. You get what the distance gives you. What the distance gave me that afternoon was two leopards in a tree and a herd of elephants below them, photographed on a mobile phone on a game drive I had not even planned to take photographs on.

I have been back to Mashatu since. I brought the camera. I will always bring the camera. But I also keep those iPhone images from 2023, because they remind me that the animal is what matters, the sighting is what matters — and that sometimes the worst preparation leads you, by accident, to the most honest photographs you have ever taken.
