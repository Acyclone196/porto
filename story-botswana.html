Tracking Brown Hyenas

The Encounter

The Tuli Block doesn't reward impatience. One of Botswana's most dramatic and untamed wilderness areas — a rugged landscape of ancient riverbeds, mopane woodland and basalt rock faces along the Limpopo — and the brown hyena, Parahyaena brunnea, is exactly the kind of animal this landscape was made to conceal. Camp Serolo, run by Tuli Wilderness, sits at the heart of it.

My wife and I had been at Camp Serolo for two nights before we saw the first tracks. Not an animal — just the impression of one in the rust-red sand at the edge of a dry pan. Wide, deliberate, unhurried. Our guide, Jou, crouched beside them without a word, ran one finger along the edge of a print, and looked up. "Last night. Maybe two, three hours before sunrise."

Three Nights Without Roads

On the second afternoon Jou found fresh tracks along a dry riverbed — wide, deliberate, pointing east. We positioned the vehicle at a bend where the tracks crossed open ground, arrived an hour before dusk, switched off the engine, and waited. The Tuli at dusk is not quiet. It hums and clicks and breathes. But it offers nothing until it decides to.

While we waited, the reserve revealed itself in layers. A lion moved through the mopane on the second night — we heard him long before we saw him, a low resonance that you feel more in the chest than the ears. A pair of klipspringers picked their way across the basalt outcrop to the west, absurdly precise in the last light. Carmine bee-eaters worked the thermals above the riverbed each morning, and on one afternoon a Verreaux's eagle crossed the sky so slowly it seemed to be considering its options.

In the Half-Light

"The hyena emerged from the dark the way a thought emerges — gradually, then all at once, already there."

She arrived just after 20:00. I know the time because I checked my watch when the torch beam caught two green eyes at the edge of the frame — and then wrote the number on the back of my hand in the way you write things when you are afraid you will forget them.

She was a large female, her long, shaggy mantle pale in the torchlight, moving with the characteristic sloping gait that makes brown hyenas look simultaneously powerful and slightly apologetic.

Reading the Silence

She moved through for close to forty minutes. We did not move, did not speak, barely breathed. In that time I made perhaps sixty frames. Most are mediocre — the light was brutal, the focus uncertain at these distances in the dark. But five frames from that sequence are among the best wildlife images I have ever made.

Why This Moment Stayed

"Wildlife photography is not about the camera, the light or the lens. It is about being willing to sit in the dark long enough."

On the last night she returned with a juvenile — smaller, still carrying the spotted flanks of a young hyena. They moved through together for nearly two hours. Jou, my wife, and I sat in silence and watched them and did not photograph. Some things deserve to be witnessed without glass in front of them.

We flew out of Jo'burg the following morning with a 512GB card and something harder to name — the particular kind of quiet that comes from having been somewhere genuinely wild.

Return · Tuli Block 2026

My wife and I returned to the Tuli Block in 2026. Different season, deeper into the dry, the riverbeds bleached white. This time the scene was different — and more violent. A gnu carcass, a few days old, had drawn her in. She was already at it when we arrived, working the hindquarters with the methodical focus of an animal that wastes nothing.
