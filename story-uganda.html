The Long Road North

Rob John met us at Entebbe with a quiet nod and a vehicle packed for more than two weeks on the road. The plan covered most of a country: Lake Victoria, Murchison Falls, Kibale, Queen Elizabeth, Bwindi, and finally back east to the Nile.

That first morning was Mabamba Swamp — a maze of papyrus channels on the edge of Lake Victoria, and one of the last reliable places on earth to find the shoebill. We slid through the channels in a low wooden canoe at first light, and after an hour the guide simply stopped paddling and pointed.

From the lake we turned north — two days at Murchison Falls, where the entire Nile forces itself through a gap seven metres wide, then south-west to Kibale Forest National Park. Kibale holds the highest density of primates in Africa. But the reason you come is the chimpanzee.

Finding the Group

The ranger located them by sound — a distant drumming, then a chorus of calls moving fast through the canopy to the north. We walked hard for twenty minutes until the trees opened slightly and there they were: a group of eleven, spread across three fig trees.

At ISO 12800 with a 500mm at f/6.3, you are asking the camera to do things it would rather not. But there are moments — when a chimp drops from one branch to another, or turns and looks directly down at you — when none of that matters.

"One of them — an adult male — dropped out of the canopy and landed ten feet away. He sat for a moment, looked at me with what I can only describe as mild irritation, and walked off into the undergrowth."

The Lions We Didn't Find

From Kibale, Rob drove us south into Queen Elizabeth National Park with one objective: the tree-climbing lions of Ishasha. We searched for two days. We found the fig trees. We found the kob herds. We did not find the lions.

Rob took it personally in the quietest possible way — a slow shake of the head at each empty fig tree, a recalculation, another loop before the light went.

Into Bwindi

From Queen Elizabeth, Rob drove us south through the Ishasha sector into the high, forested hills of Kigezi. Bwindi Impenetrable Forest is exactly what it sounds like. It holds roughly half the world's remaining mountain gorillas.

The trek to find the Mubare group took two and a half hours. Our ranger moved with the quiet economy of someone who has made this walk hundreds of times.

The Silverback

We heard the silverback before we saw him — a low chest beat, once, followed by silence. He was perhaps eight metres away, sitting with his back against a trunk, working methodically through a branch of leaves. He was enormous in the way that surprises you even when you have prepared yourself for enormity.

"He looked at us once. Not a challenge — something more like acknowledgement. Then he went back to eating. We had one hour. I spent most of it trying to remember to breathe."

Kyambura, Rwakobo & the Nile

On the way back east we dropped into Kyambura Gorge for a second chimpanzee trek. Then a night at Rwakobo Rock on the edge of Lake Mburo, zebras grazing between the granite kopjes at dusk.

The trip ended where the Nile begins. At Wildwaters Lodge, on a forested island in the middle of the river. Rob delivered us there with the same unhurried precision he had shown at the airport on day one.
