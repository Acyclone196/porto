Chandni Chowk · Old Delhi

Old Delhi does not ease you in. You step off the metro at Chandni Chowk and the city lands on you all at once — the smell of frying dough and open drains, the percussion of a thousand rickshaw bells, a man on a bicycle carrying what appears to be an entire wardrobe balanced on his head. Everything is happening at the same time, at full volume, with total indifference to whether you are ready for it.

This is Shahjahanabad — the walled city built by the Mughal emperor Shah Jahan in 1639, the same man who built the Taj Mahal. Four hundred years later the walls are mostly gone but the street logic is unchanged: narrow lanes designed for pedestrians and pack animals, now carrying a volume of motorised traffic that makes no physical sense. Cycle rickshaws, auto-rickshaws, motorcycles, handcarts, delivery trucks and the occasional cow all occupy the same two-metre-wide gali simultaneously, negotiating their passage through a system of rules that is entirely invisible to the uninitiated and appears to function perfectly.

I came to photograph people. What I found was that Old Delhi does not really give you a choice about what to photograph — it simply presents itself, continuously, faster than the eye can process it, and your job is to keep pointing the camera at the right thing at the right moment.

The spice market at Khari Baoli — the oldest spice market in Asia, in continuous operation since the seventeenth century — is where I spent most of my first morning. The streets here smell of turmeric and chilli and something I could not identify that I have since learned was asafoetida, a resin used in Indian cooking with a smell that announces its presence from three lanes away. Men sit cross-legged on jute sacks of cumin and coriander, weighing small quantities on brass hand-scales with the unhurried precision of people who have done this every day for their entire lives. Their fathers did it. Their grandfathers did it. The market itself is older than most of the countries that currently exist on earth.

What strikes you, once the initial overwhelm settles, is the coexistence. A mosque, a temple, a gurudwara and a Jain shrine within a single city block. A man reading from the Quran in a doorway while the street outside him carries a wedding procession, a funeral and a delivery of stainless steel cookware, simultaneously. Old Delhi is not a city that has learned to tolerate difference. It is a city built from it — layered over four centuries into something that cannot be reduced to a single description and resists every attempt to make it legible from the outside.

I shot for three days. On the last afternoon I was sitting on the steps of a mosque in the late light when a boy of about ten sat down beside me, looked at my camera, and said in careful English: What are you photographing? Everything, I said. He considered this. That is correct, he said, and walked away.

Sony A7 IV · Sony FE 35mm f/1.4 GM · Old Delhi, India · January 2026
