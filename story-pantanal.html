In Search of the
Onça Pintada
Pantanal · Brazil

Scroll

Pantanal · Brazil

The reason you go to the Pantanal is the jaguar. Onça pintada — the painted panther — the largest cat in the Americas, heavier than a leopard, more powerful than anything else moving through the flooded grasslands of the Pantanal. You know it is there. You go to find it.

We did not find it.

There were four of us — two friends as fascinated by wildlife as we are. We flew from Iguazú to São Paulo, then on to Campo Grande, where a driver was waiting to take us the rest of the way into the Pantanal. We split the trip between two lodges deep in the southern wetlands: first Refúgio da Ilha, then Pousada Aguapé.

The jaguar remained elusive. Tracks at the water's edge. A guide crouching to examine the freshness of a print. We were close. We were not close enough.

What we found instead was everything else the Pantanal has to offer. At Pousada Aguapé, on an early morning drive, the giant anteater — tamanduá-bandeira, the great flag-bearer — appeared on an open bank as the light came up, moving through the grass with that slow, deliberate stride that covers ground with quiet purpose. We sat with the engine off and watched it work for twenty minutes.

On a night drive, briefly — torchlight sweeping the treeline — there was a puma. A shape at the edge of the beam, a colour moving through the long grass. It was gone before anyone could hold the light on it.

The birds, however, had no such modesty. The Pantanal holds more bird species than most continents and it holds them in extraordinary density, in extraordinary light, at close range. Jabiru storks standing in the shallows with the patience of monuments. Hyacinth macaws — the deepest blue in nature — moving through the palms in pairs. Kingfishers in four different sizes. Herons and egrets and ibises in configurations that make it genuinely difficult to know where to point the lens.

We went for the painted panther. We stayed for everything else. The Pantanal does not always give you what you came for. It gives you what it has, and what it has is enough.
