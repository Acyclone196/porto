In the Land of Cats:
A Mother, a Cub, and a Kill
Mashatu Tent Camp · Northern Tuli

Into the Bush

Richard had been guiding in this concession since 1994. He knows the Northern Tuli Block the way a musician knows a piece they have played ten thousand times. That afternoon he drove slowly, reading the ground with the particular quiet of a man who does not need to announce what he is seeing.

Beside him, Commando — our tracker — was already on the move. Commando does not look for leopards. He looks for what leopards leave behind.

She Was There

Commando raised his fist and Richard cut the engine in the same breath. There she was: the nkwe, lying in the bushes, completely relaxed. She looked at us once and looked away.

Commando touched Richard on the shoulder and pointed — a few metres ahead, screened by thorn scrub, an impala. Freshly taken.

Sitting With Her

We sat. Richard kept the vehicle perfectly still, the engine off, everyone low. The mother lay close to her kill in the easy stillness of a cat with nothing left to prove that afternoon.

Then something moved in the thicket behind the kill. Smaller. Quicker. Gone before the eye could confirm.

A Flash of Gold

A flash — golden, quick, there and then not there. A cub. There was a cub.

"Thirty years in the bush and Richard called it one of the rarest sightings he knows — a mother and cub together on a fresh kill."

The Wait

Richard's hand went up and nobody moved. Then the thicket shifted. Slowly, cautiously — the cub stepped out. A nkwenyana. A young one. It studied us, decided we were not a threat, and turned toward the impala.

A Sighting of a Lifetime

The mother rose and walked to her cub. They touched — foreheads together. Lorato. Love, in Setswana. In a leopard it looks exactly like this.

Feeding Together

They fed together in the afternoon light, the cub eating with urgent focus, the mother close beside it — watchful still.

This is what thirty years with Richard and seventeen with Commando produces. To be trusted by the bush itself.

Before They Disappeared

We stayed until they were finished. When the mother finally moved off into the bush, the cub followed. The bush closed around them without a sound.

Ke ntlo ya dikgwa — this is the house of the wild.
