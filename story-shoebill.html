A Childhood Dream

There were three birds — or rather three animals — that brought me to Uganda. The chimpanzee. The mountain gorilla. And the shoebill. The first two are why most people come. The third is a private obsession: a metre-tall grey bird with the head of a pterodactyl and an expression of permanent, prehistoric disapproval. Balaeniceps rex. The king of the swamp.

Rob John knew exactly where to start. We drove to the edge of Lake Victoria and the papyrus maze of Mabamba Swamp. We slid into the channels in a low wooden canoe before the heat came up. An hour of slow paddling — and then the guide simply stopped and pointed.

It stood half-hidden among the reeds, exactly as improbable as I had imagined. And then it was gone — the moment we tried to ease the canoe closer, it lifted away across the grass.

A Swamp Full of Birds

A shoebill is the headline, but Mabamba is one of the richest patches of birdlife in Uganda. A purple heron lifted off the channel ahead of us. A pied kingfisher hung over the water and dropped, twice. A broad-billed roller watched from a bare branch.

You come for the king of the swamp. You leave realising the swamp was never only about him.

Unexpected

I had made my peace with Mabamba. One shoebill, one fleeting encounter. I did not expect a second chance.

Then, days later, on the shore of Lake Albert, Rob slowed the vehicle without a word and there it was. Another shoebill, hunting the shallows.

Closer, Then Gone

We edged closer. It let us, for a while. Then the head came up, the eye fixed on us, and the wings began to lift.

A Few Frames

This time I was ready, and I managed a few frames as it opened those vast wings and pushed off across the shallows. Two shoebills in one trip. The swamp and the lake gave me both — and took them away again just as quickly.

"Two shoebills in one trip. I had hoped, quietly, for the smallest chance of one. The swamp and the lake gave me both."
