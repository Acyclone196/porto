About the photography

Visual stories from the wild

Kirnature Photography World is a personal photography journal dedicated to capturing the beauty of nature, wildlife, landscapes, and everyday life through honest visual storytelling. Every photograph represents a quiet moment observed with patience, curiosity, and respect for the world around us.

Rather than chasing perfect scenes, Kirnature focuses on genuine experiences found in forests, mountains, rivers, villages, and the natural environments where wildlife lives freely. Each journey is an opportunity to witness moments that often go unnoticed—morning mist drifting through the trees, sunlight filtering across a valley, or the silent presence of an animal in its natural habitat.

The collection is organized into several themes, including Nature, Wildlife, Animals, and Society. Together, these galleries reflect the connection between people and the environment, showing that every place has its own atmosphere, every creature has its own story, and every journey leaves a lasting impression.

Beyond photography, Kirnature is also a space for written reflections. Many photographs are accompanied by short journal entries that share the emotions, memories, and experiences behind each image. These stories are intended to invite visitors not only to see the photographs, but also to feel the moments they represent.

Every image featured on Kirnature is created with respect for nature and wildlife. The goal is to document authentic moments without disturbing animals or altering their natural behavior, allowing every photograph to remain a truthful record of the world as it exists.

Kirnature Photography World continues to grow with every journey, every sunrise, and every new destination. It is a place to celebrate the beauty of the natural world, inspire appreciation for wildlife, and preserve meaningful moments through photography—one frame at a time.

Chandni Chowk · Old Delhi

Old Delhi does not ease you in. You step off the metro at Chandni Chowk and the city lands on you all at once — the smell of frying dough and open drains, the percussion of a thousand rickshaw bells, a man on a bicycle carrying what appears to be an entire wardrobe balanced on his head. Everything is happening at the same time, at full volume, with total indifference to whether you are ready for it.

This is Shahjahanabad — the walled city built by the Mughal emperor Shah Jahan in 1639, the same man who built the Taj Mahal. Four hundred years later the walls are mostly gone but the street logic is unchanged: narrow lanes designed for pedestrians and pack animals, now carrying a volume of motorised traffic that makes no physical sense. Cycle rickshaws, auto-rickshaws, motorcycles, handcarts, delivery trucks and the occasional cow all occupy the same two-metre-wide gali simultaneously, negotiating their passage through a system of rules that is entirely invisible to the uninitiated and appears to function perfectly.

I came to photograph people. What I found was that Old Delhi does not really give you a choice about what to photograph — it simply presents itself, continuously, faster than the eye can process it, and your job is to keep pointing the camera at the right thing at the right moment.

The spice market at Khari Baoli — the oldest spice market in Asia, in continuous operation since the seventeenth century — is where I spent most of my first morning. The streets here smell of turmeric and chilli and something I could not identify that I have since learned was asafoetida, a resin used in Indian cooking with a smell that announces its presence from three lanes away. Men sit cross-legged on jute sacks of cumin and coriander, weighing small quantities on brass hand-scales with the unhurried precision of people who have done this every day for their entire lives. Their fathers did it. Their grandfathers did it. The market itself is older than most of the countries that currently exist on earth.

What strikes you, once the initial overwhelm settles, is the coexistence. A mosque, a temple, a gurudwara and a Jain shrine within a single city block. A man reading from the Quran in a doorway while the street outside him carries a wedding procession, a funeral and a delivery of stainless steel cookware, simultaneously. Old Delhi is not a city that has learned to tolerate difference. It is a city built from it — layered over four centuries into something that cannot be reduced to a single description and resists every attempt to make it legible from the outside.

I shot for three days. On the last afternoon I was sitting on the steps of a mosque in the late light when a boy of about ten sat down beside me, looked at my camera, and said in careful English: What are you photographing? Everything, I said. He considered this. That is correct, he said, and walked away.

Tracking Brown Hyenas
in the Tuli Block
Camp Serolo · Tuli Wilderness

The Encounter

The Tuli Block doesn't reward impatience. One of Botswana's most dramatic and untamed wilderness areas — a rugged landscape of ancient riverbeds, mopane woodland and basalt rock faces along the Limpopo — and the brown hyena, Parahyaena brunnea, is exactly the kind of animal this landscape was made to conceal. Camp Serolo, run by Tuli Wilderness, sits at the heart of it.

My wife and I had been at Camp Serolo for two nights before we saw the first tracks. Not an animal — just the impression of one in the rust-red sand at the edge of a dry pan. Wide, deliberate, unhurried. Our guide, Jou, crouched beside them without a word, ran one finger along the edge of a print, and looked up. "Last night. Maybe two, three hours before sunrise."

Brown hyenas are classified as near threatened. They are rarely photographed in the wild — not because they are especially rare within their range, but because they are almost entirely nocturnal, intensely shy, and extraordinarily good at disappearing into terrain that itself seems designed for disappearing.

Three Nights Without Roads

On the second afternoon Jou found fresh tracks along a dry riverbed — wide, deliberate, pointing east. We positioned the vehicle at a bend where the tracks crossed open ground, arrived an hour before dusk, switched off the engine, and waited. The Tuli at dusk is not quiet. It hums and clicks and breathes. But it offers nothing until it decides to.

While we waited, the reserve revealed itself in layers. A lion moved through the mopane on the second night — we heard him long before we saw him, a low resonance that you feel more in the chest than the ears. A pair of klipspringers picked their way across the basalt outcrop to the west, absurdly precise in the last light. Carmine bee-eaters worked the thermals above the riverbed each morning, and on one afternoon a Verreaux's eagle crossed the sky so slowly it seemed to be considering its options. None of this was why we came. All of it became part of the photographic memory of that week.

I was shooting with the Sony A7 IV — the 200–600mm on a beanbag for the distance work, the 70–200mm close at hand for anything that came nearer. I had pre-focused on the crossing point in the sand and set a manual exposure I could adjust in the dark without looking at the camera — ISO 12800, f/5.6, 1/160s. Enough to freeze movement in red-light torch conditions. Not enough to feel comfortable about.

In the Half-Light

"The hyena emerged from the dark the way a thought emerges — gradually, then all at once, already there."

She arrived just after 20:00. I know the time because I checked my watch when the torch beam caught two green eyes at the edge of the frame — and then wrote the number on the back of my hand in the way you write things when you are afraid you will forget them.

She was a large female, her long, shaggy mantle pale in the torchlight, moving with the characteristic sloping gait that makes brown hyenas look simultaneously powerful and slightly apologetic. She crossed the open ground in three wide arcs before committing — head low, ears rotating — reading the air for anything that shouldn't be there.

Reading the Silence

She moved through for close to forty minutes. We did not move, did not speak, barely breathed. In that time I made perhaps sixty frames. Most are mediocre — the light was brutal, the focus uncertain at these distances in the dark. But five frames from that sequence are among the best wildlife images I have ever made. Not technically. Technically they are imperfect. But emotionally — in the thing they hold — they are exactly right.

Why This Moment Stayed

"Wildlife photography is not about the camera, the light or the lens. It is about being willing to sit in the dark long enough."

On the last night she returned with a juvenile — smaller, still carrying the spotted flanks of a young hyena. They moved through together for nearly two hours, pausing, testing the air, disappearing into the mopane and reappearing somewhere unexpected. Jou, my wife, and I sat in silence and watched them and did not photograph. Some things deserve to be witnessed without glass in front of them.

We flew out of Jo'burg the following morning with a 512GB card and something harder to name — the particular kind of quiet that comes from having been somewhere genuinely wild. The Tuli Block gives you back exactly what you bring to it. Patience. Stillness. Attention. And occasionally, just after eight in the evening, green eyes in the dark.

Return · Tuli Block 2026

My wife and I returned to the Tuli Block in 2026. Different season, deeper into the dry, the riverbeds bleached white. This time the scene was different — and more violent. A gnu carcass, a few days old, had drawn her in. She was already at it when we arrived, working the hindquarters with the methodical focus of an animal that wastes nothing. Then she stopped, gripped a rear leg at the joint, and simply walked off with it into the mopane. No hesitation, no ceremony — just the leg, swinging, and the dark swallowing her whole. That is the image in the 2026 frames: not just a hyena, but a hyena going somewhere, with intention, carrying what she came for.

Into the Bush

Richard had been guiding in this concession since 1994. He knows the Northern Tuli Block the way a musician knows a piece they have played ten thousand times — not from memory, but from the body. That afternoon he drove slowly, reading the ground with the particular quiet of a man who does not need to announce what he is seeing.

Beside him, Commando — our tracker — was already on the move. Commando does not look for leopards. He looks for what leopards leave behind: the pressed sand, the turned stone, the dry grass that bends the wrong way. Between the two of them, very little in this bush gets missed.

She Was There

Commando raised his fist and Richard cut the engine in the same breath — a single fluid motion built from years of working together. There she was: the nkwe, lying in the bushes, completely relaxed, the way leopards are when they know the ground belongs to them. She looked at us once and looked away.

Commando touched Richard on the shoulder and pointed — a few metres ahead, screened by thorn scrub, an impala. Freshly taken. She had eaten in the night and come to rest nearby. Richard turned to us and said nothing. He didn't need to.

Sitting With Her

We sat. Richard kept the vehicle perfectly still, the engine off, everyone low. He has a gift for creating silence around an animal — not just the absence of noise, but a kind of presence that doesn't disturb. The mother lay close to her kill in the easy stillness of a cat with nothing left to prove that afternoon.

Then something moved in the thicket behind the kill. Smaller. Quicker. Gone before the eye could confirm what the heart already understood. Richard's hand went up — wait.

A Flash of Gold

A flash — golden, quick, there and then not there. Smaller than the mother. Lighter on its feet. Richard glanced at Commando. Commando nodded slowly. They had both seen it at the same moment and understood what it was before any of us did.

A cub. There was a cub.

"Thirty years in the bush and Richard called it one of the rarest sightings he knows — a mother and cub together on a fresh kill. Some afternoons the bush saves its best for last."

The Wait

Richard's hand went up and nobody moved. In the African bush, stillness is the only currency that buys you anything — and Richard and Commando between them have spent a lifetime earning it. Commando had not moved in four minutes. Richard watched the thicket with the focused patience of a man who knows that forcing the moment always costs you the moment.

Then the thicket shifted. Slowly, cautiously — the cub stepped out. A nkwenyana. A young one. It studied us with eyes that were not yet sure what we were, decided we were not a threat, and turned toward the impala. Commando exhaled. It was the quietest celebration I have ever heard.

"The bush does not take sides. It simply continues. Something dies so something else may live — and both of them, in the end, belong to the same story."

A Sighting of a Lifetime

The mother rose and walked to her cub in that long, flowing way leopards have of covering ground without appearing to hurry. They touched — foreheads together, the rumble of something deep in her chest that we felt more than heard. Lorato. Love, in Setswana. In a leopard it looks exactly like this.

Richard turned to look at us. Thirty years in these hills, and even for him this was something rare — a mother and cub together on a fresh kill, close enough to photograph, staying. He said nothing. His expression said everything.

Feeding Together

They fed together in the afternoon light, the cub eating with the urgent focus of youth, the mother close beside it — watchful still, because the instinct that keeps a cub alive never fully rests, even on an afternoon like this. Commando watched them the way he watches everything: completely, quietly, without disturbing the air between him and the animals.

This is what thirty years with Richard and seventeen with Commando produces. Not just access to the animals — the ability to stay with them. To be trusted by the bush itself.

Before They Disappeared

We stayed until they were finished. Nobody wanted to leave. When the mother finally moved off into the jesse bush, the cub followed — one step behind, already learning the shape of her. The bush closed around them without a sound.

On the drive back Richard said very little. Commando looked out at the passing mopane with a small, private smile. Between them, no words were needed. This was their ground, their animals, their afternoon — and they had chosen to share it with us.

Ke ntlo ya dikgwa — this is the house of the wild. We were guests. And for one afternoon, the wild let us see everything.

The Year I Left
the Camera Behind
Mashatu · Northern Tuli · 2023

Scroll

Mashatu Tent Camp · Main Camp · Tuli Safari Lodge · Northern Tuli Block · 2023

I made a decision before the 2023 trip. A conscious, considered, deliberate decision that I had been thinking about for a long time. I was going to leave the Sony at home. No 200–600mm. No heavy bag. No compulsion to be behind a lens every time something extraordinary happened in front of me. I was going to be present. I was going to immerse myself in the bush — really immerse myself — and experience it the way it deserves to be experienced, without glass between me and the world.

It was, as it turned out, one of the most spectacular own goals of my photographic career.

The trip had not started simply. We arrived at Mashatu Tent Camp to find that the temperatures had become extreme — the kind of heat that the Northern Tuli Block occasionally produces in certain seasons, the kind that makes a tented camp genuinely uncomfortable and, for the management, untenable. Mashatu moved us. We spent part of the stay at Mashatu Main Camp, the older and more substantial lodge on the same reserve, where the solid walls and deeper shade offered some relief from the relentless sun. After Main Camp we continued to our original booking at Tuli Safari Lodge, where we finished the trip. Three camps, one reserve, one exceptional guide threading us through all of it.

Dan and his tracker Morris have been reading this landscape together for years — the leopard territories, the elephant routes, the way the dry season concentrates animals around specific water sources. On the drive that changed the trip, they found us two leopards in a tree with an entire herd of elephants moving slowly beneath them. Engine off. Nobody speaking. The elephants knew the leopards were there. The leopards knew the elephants were there. Nobody was interested in confrontation — this is the Northern Tuli Block, where the animals share the ground with the pragmatic ease of long familiarity. Dan had positioned us exactly where we needed to be to see it, the way he always does.

I had my iPhone.

Here is what I have learned about iPhone photography in the African bush: it forces you to be honest. You cannot reach across fifty metres with a long lens and isolate a face in shallow depth of field. You get what the distance gives you. What the distance gave me that afternoon was two leopards in a tree and a herd of elephants below them, photographed on a mobile phone on a game drive I had not even planned to take photographs on. The limitations of the phone became irrelevant. I was pointing it at something extraordinary.

I have been back to Mashatu since. I brought the camera. I will always bring the camera. But I also keep those iPhone images from 2023, because they remind me that the animal is what matters, the sighting is what matters — and that sometimes the worst preparation leads you, by accident, to the most honest photographs you have ever taken.

Dan turns 54 this October. I hope he is still out there finding leopards for people who did not bring enough camera.

Pantanal · Brazil

The reason you go to the Pantanal is the jaguar. Onça pintada — the painted panther — the largest cat in the Americas, heavier than a leopard, more powerful than anything else moving through the flooded grasslands of the Pantanal. You have seen the photographs. The great spotted cat on the riverbank, its reflection perfect in the black water. You know it is there. You go to find it.

We did not find it.

There were four of us — two friends as fascinated by wildlife as we are, the kind of people with whom you can spend an entire afternoon watching a single bird without anyone suggesting it might be time to move on. We flew from Iguazú to São Paulo, then on to Campo Grande, where a driver was waiting to take us the rest of the way into the Pantanal. We split the trip between two lodges deep in the southern wetlands: first Refúgio da Ilha, then Pousada Aguapé. Both sit inside private reserves; both operate the kind of guided game drives and boat excursions that make the most of the extraordinary density of life around them.

The jaguar remained elusive. Tracks at the water's edge. A guide crouching to examine the freshness of a print. A long, flat stretch of river where something large had very recently been — the grass still bent, the mud still soft. We were close. We were not close enough.

What we found instead was everything else the Pantanal has to offer, which turns out to be considerable. At Pousada Aguapé, on an early morning drive, the giant anteater — tamanduá-bandeira, the great flag-bearer — appeared on an open bank as the light came up, moving through the grass with that slow, deliberate stride that looks unhurried but covers ground with quiet purpose. We sat with the engine off and watched it work for twenty minutes. It did not acknowledge us. It was entirely absorbed in its own business, the long curved snout sweeping low across the termite mounds, the extraordinary shaggy tail trailing behind like something borrowed from another animal entirely. It is not a jaguar. In almost every way that does not involve a jaguar, it is better than a jaguar.

On a night drive, briefly — torchlight sweeping the treeline, the engine idling — there was a puma. A shape at the edge of the beam, a colour moving through the long grass, a presence that registered before the conscious mind had time to name it. It was gone before anyone could hold the light on it. That is usually how it goes with pumas. They have no interest in being seen and they are extremely good at not being seen. We sat for a while in the dark after it left, saying nothing. There was nothing there. There had been something there.

The birds, however, had no such modesty.

The Pantanal holds more bird species than most continents and it holds them in extraordinary density, in extraordinary light, at close range. Jabiru storks standing in the shallows with the patience of monuments. Hyacinth macaws — the deepest blue in nature, a colour that seems impossible until you are looking directly at it — moving through the palms in pairs, unhurried and unhurriable. Kingfishers in four different sizes, each occupying a different rung on the same perch. Herons and egrets and ibises in configurations that make it genuinely difficult to know where to point the lens. The photography was relentless in the best possible way: too many subjects, too much light, not enough time.

We went for the painted panther. We stayed for everything else. The Pantanal does not always give you what you came for. It gives you what it has, and what it has is enough.

The Long Road North

Rob John met us at Entebbe with a quiet nod and a vehicle packed for more than two weeks on the road. The plan covered most of a country: Lake Victoria, Murchison Falls, Kibale, Queen Elizabeth, Bwindi, and finally back east to the Nile. He had driven every kilometre of it before, many times, and it showed from the first morning.

That first morning was Mabamba Swamp — a maze of papyrus channels on the edge of Lake Victoria, and one of the last reliable places on earth to find the shoebill. We slid through the channels in a low wooden canoe at first light, and after an hour the guide simply stopped paddling and pointed. The bird stood motionless among the reeds, prehistoric and improbable, staring down at the water with the patience of something that has been doing exactly this for fifty million years.

From the lake we turned north — two days at Murchison Falls, where the entire Nile forces itself through a gap seven metres wide, then the long road south-west to Kibale Forest National Park. Kibale holds the highest density of primates in Africa: thirteen species in a single forest. But the reason you come is the chimpanzee — Pan troglodytes, our closest living relative — and the reason I had brought a 500mm lens and considerably less certainty than I usually bring to a shoot.

Finding the Group

The ranger located them by sound — a distant drumming, then a chorus of calls moving fast through the canopy to the north. We walked hard for twenty minutes, following trails that narrowed to nothing, until the trees opened slightly and there they were: a group of eleven, spread across three fig trees, feeding with complete indifference to our presence below.

Shooting in a closed forest canopy is a technical challenge that no amount of preparation entirely solves. The light is filtered, broken, constantly shifting. At ISO 12800 with a 500mm at f/6.3, you are asking the camera to do things it would rather not. But there are moments — when a chimp drops from one branch to another, or turns and looks directly down at you with an expression that is uncomfortably familiar — when none of that matters. You press the shutter because the image exists and you happen to be there.

"One of them — an adult male — dropped out of the canopy and landed ten feet away. He sat for a moment, looked at me with what I can only describe as mild irritation, and walked off into the undergrowth."

The Lions We Didn't Find

From Kibale, Rob drove us south into Queen Elizabeth National Park with one objective: the tree-climbing lions of Ishasha. It is one of only two populations in the world known to rest routinely in trees — sprawled along the limbs of giant fig trees through the heat of the day. We searched for two days. We found the fig trees. We found the kob herds the lions feed on, elephants crossing the Kazinga Channel, hippos stacked along the banks like cargo. We did not find the lions.

Rob took it personally in the quietest possible way — a slow shake of the head at each empty fig tree, a recalculation, another loop of the sector before the light went. Uganda teaches patience the way all good places do: by simply not cooperating until you stop expecting it to. Some animals you earn. Some you are owed for next time.

Into Bwindi

From Queen Elizabeth, Rob drove us south through the Ishasha sector and on into the high, forested hills of Kigezi — a long road through tea plantations and small towns, climbing steadily out of the Rift Valley. Bwindi Impenetrable Forest is exactly what it sounds like. Dense, steep, trackless in every direction that isn't a carefully maintained ranger path. It holds roughly half the world's remaining mountain gorillas — perhaps 460 individuals across several habituated groups.

The trek to find the Mubare group took two and a half hours. The terrain was steep and wet, the vegetation closing in until visibility was sometimes less than ten metres. Our ranger moved with the quiet economy of someone who has made this walk hundreds of times and still pays attention every single step.

The Silverback

We heard the silverback before we saw him — a low chest beat, once, followed by silence. The ranger stopped, held up one hand, and we stood motionless for perhaps two minutes before she gestured us quietly forward.

He was perhaps eight metres away, sitting with his back against a trunk, working methodically through a branch of leaves. He was enormous in the way that surprises you even when you have prepared yourself for enormity. The family was spread around him in a loose semicircle — two adult females, three juveniles, a small infant clinging to its mother's back and watching us with enormous dark eyes.

"He looked at us once. Not a challenge — something more like acknowledgement. Then he went back to eating. We had one hour. I spent most of it trying to remember to breathe."

Kyambura, Rwakobo & the Nile

The journey wasn't finished with the gorillas. On the way back east we dropped into Kyambura Gorge — a sunken ribbon of rainforest cut a hundred metres into the savannah floor — for a second chimpanzee trek, this time scrambling down steep, root-laced paths with the calls echoing off the gorge walls above us. Then a night at Rwakobo Rock on the edge of Lake Mburo, zebras grazing between the granite kopjes at dusk.

The trip ended where the Nile begins. At Wildwaters Lodge, on a forested island in the middle of the river, the white water ran on both sides of the room and the sound of it never stopped. After more than two weeks and a couple of thousand kilometres, Rob delivered us there with the same unhurried precision he had shown at the airport on day one. That is what you learn, travelling with someone who knows the land — not the headline animals, but the texture of a place. Rob John carries Uganda in his head with the same quiet authority that Jou carries the Tuli Block: the knowledge of a life spent paying attention to one piece of the world, and finding it inexhaustible.

A Childhood Dream

There were three birds — or rather three animals — that brought me to Uganda. The chimpanzee. The mountain gorilla. And the shoebill. The first two are why most people come. The third is a private obsession, the kind of thing you read about as a child and file away as improbable: a metre-tall grey bird with the head of a pterodactyl and an expression of permanent, prehistoric disapproval. Balaeniceps rex. The king of the swamp.

Rob John knew exactly where to start. Before the chimps, before the gorillas, before any of it, we drove to the edge of Lake Victoria and the papyrus maze of Mabamba Swamp — one of the last reliable strongholds of the shoebill anywhere on earth. We slid into the channels in a low wooden canoe before the heat came up, the water black and still, the papyrus closing overhead. An hour of slow paddling and quiet scanning — and then the guide simply stopped and pointed.

It stood half-hidden among the reeds, exactly as improbable as I had imagined, staring down at the water with the patience of something that has been doing this for fifty million years. And then it was gone — the moment we tried to ease the canoe closer, it turned, opened those enormous wings and lifted away across the grass. There are far better shoebill photographs in the world than the ones I came home with. But they are mine, and they are that morning: a childhood dream, standing in the reeds, looking back.

A Swamp Full of Birds

A shoebill is the headline, but Mabamba is one of the richest patches of birdlife in Uganda, and the canoe drifted past one species after another in the early light. A purple heron lifted off the channel ahead of us on slow, deliberate wings. A pied kingfisher hung over the water and dropped, twice, before settling on a papyrus stem to swallow its catch. A broad-billed roller watched the whole performance from a bare branch, lilac-throated and entirely unbothered.

You come for the king of the swamp. You leave realising the swamp was never only about him.

Unexpected

I had made my peace with Mabamba. One shoebill, one fleeting encounter, a dream ticked off in the most honest way possible — seen, briefly, on its own terms. I did not expect a second chance. They are not a bird you stumble upon twice.

Then, days later and a country away, on the shore of Lake Albert in the west, Rob slowed the vehicle without a word and there it was. Another shoebill, hunting the shallows, utterly unbothered by the lake behind it and entirely bothered by us.

Closer, Then Gone

This time there was no canoe and no papyrus to hide behind — just open shoreline and a bird that knew exactly how much distance it wanted to keep. We edged closer. It let us, for a while, hunting and preening as if we weren't there. Then the head came up, the eye fixed on us, and the wings began to lift — the universal shoebill signal that the audience is over.

A Few Frames

It was just as elusive as the first — the same instinct to put distance between itself and the world the instant you commit to it. But this time I was ready, and I managed a few frames as it opened those vast wings and pushed off across the shallows. Not the best shoebill photographs ever made. Just mine, of a second bird I had no right to expect.

"Two shoebills in one trip. I had hoped, quietly, for the smallest chance of one. The swamp and the lake gave me both — and took them away again just as quickly. That is the deal with a shoebill, and it is a fair one."

Dan & Morris

TrackerMorris

There are guides who find animals and guides who find the right animals at the right moment. Dan is the second kind. He guides at Mashatu Tent Camp in the Northern Tuli Block with the particular calm authority of someone who knows their ground not just professionally but personally — the way the light changes on the basalt koppies through the seasons, the territories of the cats, the water points the elephants favour in dry years. He has spent years learning a landscape that rewards exactly that kind of sustained attention.

I met Dan in 2023, on the trip I will always call the iPhone year. I had decided to leave my Sony at home — a decision made with the best of intentions and the worst of outcomes, photographically speaking. Dan, with his tracker Morris, proceeded to deliver some of the finest leopard sightings I have had anywhere on the continent. Several days running. Cats in koppies, cats in rivers, cats on kills, cats simply being perfect cats in perfect light, for an audience of one person holding a mobile phone and trying not to say anything he would regret.

What I remember most about Dan is his steadiness. He does not get excited in the way that is performative — the kind of excitement that is really for the guests rather than the animals. His excitement, when it comes, is quiet and genuine, a slight increase in focus and attention that tells you something worth seeing is about to happen. He communicates with Morris the same way — small signals, minimal words, a shared language built over many drives through the same ground. They work as a unit the way good partnerships always do: each knowing what the other is thinking before it is said.

The Northern Tuli Block in 2023 was, for the leopards at least, an extraordinary year. Dan and Morris found them as if they were following a schedule. Whether that was down to the season, the animals' movements, or simply two people who understood their territory at a level that made luck look like skill — I cannot say. What I can say is that on a trip I had deliberately designed to be camera-free, Dan gave me more to photograph than almost any trip I can remember.

Photographer's Note · John Janson The image on my iPhone of two leopards in a tree with an elephant herd moving beneath them — technically modest by any standard, shot from the distance a mobile phone imposes — is one of my favourite images from that year. Not because of what the phone could do. Because of where Dan put us. The credit is his.

Jou

Serolo Camp · Tuli Wilderness · Botswana

Jou is one of those guides you do not forget. Not because of the animals he finds — though he finds them — but because of how he changes the way you look at the land around you. By the end of a drive with Jou, you are not scanning the bush for movement. You are reading it.

At 63, he continues to guide through the Tuli Block with the same physical commitment and focus you would expect from someone half his age. This is not a career winding down. It is a career at full depth — every year of experience compounding into something that simply cannot be taught in a classroom or replicated from a field guide.

His relationship with the Tuli Block goes beyond professional knowledge. This landscape — the ancient riverbeds, the basalt outcrops, the mopane woodland, the dry pans — is territory he has spent a lifetime learning. He knows where the brown hyenas move after dark. He knows which pan the lion uses at first light. He knows when the bee-eaters will come back to the riverbank and which direction to look when the Verreaux's eagle crosses the sky.

Behind the guiding is a father — a man who has spent his career not just tracking wildlife but providing, quietly and without complaint, for the people he loves. Guests leave the Tuli Block with photographs and memories. Jou stays, and gets up the next morning and does it again. His resilience is not something he mentions. You simply feel it — in the steadiness of his attention, in the way he reads a situation before it develops, in the way he is always, somehow, already where he needs to be.

Photographer's Note · John Janson A good safari guide changes the way you see. With Jou, every drive in the Tuli Block became a lesson in patience, attention and reading the land. He did not just find the hyena — he taught me how to wait for her. The images from those nights exist because of his skill, his knowledge of this specific piece of Africa, and his ability to be completely still when stillness was required. I owe him several of my best wildlife frames.

Safari Guide & Tracker · Mashatu Tent Camp · Northern Tuli · Botswana

Richard & Commando

Mashatu Tent Camp · Mashatu · Botswana

Guiding since 1994 Big Cats Elephant Behaviour Track & Sign Leopard Born · Mashatu 1966

Richard was born in 1966 in the Northern Tuli Block — not near this landscape, but inside it. Before Mashatu Tent Camp had a name that appeared in travel magazines, before the game drives were scheduled and the guests arrived with their lenses, this land was simply where he grew up. He knows the dry seasons by how they smell and the wet seasons by which pans flood first. He knows the movement of the elephant herds the way a man knows the rhythms of his own neighbourhood — not from study, but from a lifetime of paying attention.

He started guiding in 1994. That is thirty years in the same piece of Botswana — thirty years of watching the same landscape change season by season, of tracking the same predator corridors, of learning which individual animals use which routes at which times of year. This is not a generalist's knowledge. This is the deep, specific intelligence that only one place can produce in one person, over a very long time.

At 59, Richard reads the bush the way a musician reads a score — fluidly, ahead of the beat, already knowing what is coming before it arrives. He sees the elephant's body language before the elephant has decided what to do. He positions the vehicle before the light has shifted. He is, in the specific technical sense, already where he needs to be.

A father of three, he carries the same unhurried steadiness into family as he does into the field. The reserve is not just his workplace. It is the ground he was born on, and he moves through it with the particular ease of someone who has never once been lost in it.

Photographer's Note · John Janson Richard saw the leopard before I did. He saw the elephant change direction before I understood why the vehicle had moved. He had already framed the shot — in the sense of choosing the ground, the angle, the moment to stop — before I had raised the camera. Thirty years in this specific piece of Africa has produced something that no training course produces and no field guide contains. I owe him most of what I came back with.

Tracker · Mashatu Tent Camp · Northern Tuli · Botswana

Commando

Then there is Commando. The tracker. The man who works the ground ahead of the vehicle, reading what the land says to those who know how to listen.

At 45, Commando has been tracking at Mashatu since 2008 — seventeen years of following animals through one of southern Africa's most demanding terrains. Basalt koppies that hold no prints. Dry riverbeds where the sand tells everything. Open mopane where the dust shifts with the wind and the track that was there an hour ago is half a story by midday. He reads all of it.

Watching Commando work is watching someone in complete mastery of a discipline that is almost impossible to teach. You either develop the eye for it over years of concentrated attention, or you do not. He crouches beside a print in the sand and tells you not just what animal made it, but how recently, how fast it was moving, whether it was alert or relaxed, and which direction it was heading as a matter of intent rather than momentum. He reconstructs the last few hours of an animal's life from the evidence it leaves without meaning to.

A father of three — two boys and a girl — Commando is quiet in the way people are quiet when they are thinking about something more important than conversation. In the vehicle he communicates mostly in the small economy of gesture: a pointed finger, a held breath, a tilt of the head that says there a full beat before your eyes have found anything. He and Richard work together with the ease of people who have been doing so for years — each reading what the other is reading, without needing to say it aloud.

Photographer's Note · John Janson The leopard on the riverbank — the image that made the trip — exists because of Commando. He found the tracks. He read the direction. He understood where she had come from and where she was going, and he put us in front of it before she arrived. I was pointing the camera. He had already done everything else.

Driver Guide · Uganda

Rob John

Mabamba · Murchison Falls · Kibale · Queen Elizabeth · Bwindi · Kyambura · The Nile

Chimpanzee Trekking Gorilla Trekking Shoebill · Mabamba Kibale Forest Bwindi Uganda Roads Wildlife Logistics

There are guides who find animals, and there are guides who find everything else — the road that doesn't flood, the ranger who will give you an extra thirty minutes with the gorilla family, the lodge that has two rooms left on a Tuesday in October. Rob John is the second kind, and the reason a trip through Uganda works is often that he is the one who made it work.

Rob drove us across Uganda for more than two weeks — from the papyrus channels of Mabamba Swamp on Lake Victoria, north to Murchison Falls, south-west to Kibale for the chimpanzees, through Queen Elizabeth in search of the tree-climbing lions of Ishasha, into the hills of Bwindi for the gorillas, down into Kyambura Gorge for a second chimp trek, past the kopjes of Rwakobo Rock at Lake Mburo, and finally east to an island in the Nile at Wildwaters Lodge. Long roads through tea country and small market towns, through rain and road works and the particular chaos of a border district at dusk. He drove all of it with the easy competence of someone who has been doing it for years and has long since stopped finding it interesting to mention. What he finds interesting are the animals. He knows the country the way a city person knows their neighbourhood — not just the landmarks, but the texture, the timing, the things that are slightly out of place.

At Kibale he introduced us to the ranger who would lead our trek, spoke to him quietly in Luganda for a few minutes, and then said: "He knows where the family went last night. We'll find them early." We did. At Bwindi he parked the vehicle at a specific point on the road above the park and said nothing for a moment. Then: "Sometimes they come up here. Not today, but the tracks were fresh this morning." He had checked before we arrived. That is the kind of attention that looks effortless because it is so consistently applied.

Uganda is a complicated country to move through quickly — the distances are real, the roads unpredictable, the logistics of permit timings and ranger briefings and gate openings genuinely difficult to coordinate. Rob coordinated all of it and made it feel, from the passenger seat, as though it had simply arranged itself. That is a specific skill, and it is not a small one.

Photographer's Note · John Janson Rob John is not a wildlife guide in the traditional sense — he is a driver and logistics expert who happens to know Uganda better than almost anyone I have travelled with. Without him, the Uganda trip would have been possible but considerably less. With him, it was seamless: the chimps were found, the gorilla permits were timed correctly, the light was right at Bwindi because we arrived when we were supposed to, not twenty minutes late. Behind every good photograph in a difficult country is someone who knew the road.
